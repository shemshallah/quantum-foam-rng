Enterprise-Grade Quantum Randomness

OaGI Quantum RNG implements a rigorous multi-basis measurement protocol for generating cryptographically secure random numbers using quantum mechanical processes. Our system leverages entangled Bell states measured across nine complementary bases (ZZ, XX, YY, ZX, XZ, ZY, YZ, XY, YX) to extract maximal quantum entropy.

Currently deployed on IonQ's quantum simulator with plans to transition to live quantum hardware as funding becomes available. This approach has been benchmarked against leading protocols in quantum random number generation literature, including the Joo et al. framework and other Bell-state-based QRNG implementations.

Technical Implementation

  • Architecture: Multi-basis Bell state measurement protocol
  • Entanglement Source: Parameterized Bell states with controlled rotation angles
  • Measurement Bases: 9 complementary Pauli basis pairs (ZZ, XX, YY, ZX, XZ, ZY, YZ, XY, YX)
  • Extraction Method: Von Neumann debiasing + Toeplitz hashing
  • Output: 256-bit cryptographic keys
  • Hardware: IonQ trapped-ion quantum simulator (production: IonQ Aria)
  • Parallel Optimization: Concurrent circuit execution across all measurement bases
  • Security: Quantum-certified randomness with information-theoretic guarantees

The Quantum Transition: Challenges & Opportunities

Post-Quantum Cryptography Crisis

Current encryption standards (RSA, ECC) face existential threats from quantum computers. Organizations must transition to quantum-resistant cryptography while the infrastructure remains vulnerable. Quantum RNG provides a bridge: cryptographic keys generated from quantum sources remain secure even against quantum adversaries.

Hardware Access & Cost Barriers

Real quantum hardware remains expensive and access-limited. Cloud quantum services charge per shot, making large-scale deployment costly. Our simulator implementation enables development and testing, but production systems require funding to access live quantum processors like IonQ Aria for deployment-grade security guarantees.

Trust & Verification

How do users verify randomness is truly quantum? Classical simulations can mimic quantum statistics. Our solution: Bell inequality testing provides cryptographic proof of quantum origin. CHSH > 2 violations are physically impossible for classical sources, creating verifiable quantum certificates.
